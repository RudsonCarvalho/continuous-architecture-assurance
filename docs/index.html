01

The problem that started it

We are starting to let AI AI = Artificial Intelligence review our architecture and code decisions. When an AI reviewer says "this design has a retry-storm risk," what happens next? In most teams, one of two things: they trust it and act, or they ignore it as noise. Both are failures of the same kind — neither is based on a measurement of how reliable that reviewer is on that kind of problem.

The central analogy. We would never accept this from a physical instrument. A thermometer that has never been calibrated is not a thermometer — it is an opinion with a number on it. Yet we deploy AI reviewers, which are far less stable than thermometers, and accept their readings on faith.

And "add more reviewers" doesn't fix it. It becomes what I call checklist theater: a volume of plausible-sounding feedback that nobody arbitrates, often built on failure scenarios the model simply invented — accepted because the reviewers are presumed competent. Ten unreliable instruments don't add up to one reliable one. They add up to more noise with a quorum.

The real question

It's not "how do we get more AI review?" It's: when an AI takes part in judging whether a system is sound, what assures the judge? Today, the honest answer in most organizations is silence, or vendor trust.

02

The idea: treat the reviewer as an instrument

The move at the heart of CAA is to stop treating AI reviewers as oracles (sources believed on authority) and start treating them as instruments that must be calibrated. This single reframing imports a century of mature practice from metrology metrology = the science of measurement and calibration.

The consequence that makes this powerful: CAA is ruler-agnostic. It doesn't compete with your well-architected framework, your maturity model, or your AI reviewers — any of them plugs in as an instrument, provided it agrees to be calibrated and to emit findings under contract. That's why this space was empty when I looked: whoever sells a ruler has no incentive to build the mechanism that can fail their own ruler.

03

The two planes and the rule that doesn't bend

CAA separates a production plane — whatever produces code and decisions, be it an AI pipeline, a software factory, or a human team — from an assurance plane assurance = independent guarantee / vouching, which consumes claims and evidence and emits auditable confidence. Between them, a structured verdict contract.

The golden rule

The party that assures cannot be the party that produces. A production pipeline's own reviewers and internal gates are part of production — CAA audits them too. It's the same logic as internal controls versus independent audit at a bank: the first doesn't remove the need for the second, and the regulator requires both.

Hold onto this rule. By the end of the saga, the experiment will have proven, five times, why it is the load-bearing wall of the whole design.

04

How we measure a reviewer

A reviewer is calibrated on two numbers that must always travel together:

Recall recall = hit rate / sensitivity — of the real failures, how many did the reviewer catch? A reviewer that misses real risks is useless.

False-positive rate FP = false positive: an alarm where there was no danger — on healthy systems, how often did the reviewer cry wolf? A reviewer that flags everything is also useless — it just shifts the cost to the human who has to filter the noise.

The pair is the whole point. Either number alone is gameable. A reviewer that flags everything has perfect recall — and is useless. A reviewer that approves everything has zero false positives — and is useless. Only the pair exposes both. This is the claim the experiment set out to break.

Youden

To summarize both numbers into one, we use the Youden index Youden = recall − false-positive; higher is a better instrument. A perfect reviewer scores 1.0; one that flags everything or approves everything tends to 0.

05

What we measured against — the corpus

I couldn't calibrate against opinions, so I calibrated against reality: public postmortems postmortem = a public report analyzing a real incident of real outages from AWS, GitHub, Cloudflare, CircleCI, Mozilla, Twilio, Val Town and others. For each incident, I wrote a matched pair:

Each case gets one of 12 root-cause categories (a fixed taxonomy of recurring failure modes — retry without idempotency, mis-calibrated timeout, pool exhaustion, deploy without safe rollback, and so on). And each case has a distractor: a plausible-but-false cause, planted on purpose. A reviewer that pattern-matches surface symptoms bites the distractor; one that reasons about the mechanism reaches the true cause.

Blinding

A case is only useful if the reviewer has to reason to the answer rather than read it off the page. So each case passed a "blind reader" test: no sentence may state the conclusion, and no category code may appear in the text. This didn't come out right the first time — the final corpus is the third audited rewrite; earlier versions leaked the answer and were fixed.

06

The saga: five attempts to break it

This is the part most reports hide. I put it at the center, because the dead ends are the most valuable content — each one shows how easily an AI-review process can fool you, and what it took to catch it. I did not try to confirm CAA; I tried to falsify it falsify = try to prove it wrong, in the scientific sense.

1

The control that refused to be bad

automated verdict: "falsified" — but wrong

I built two real reviewers and two deliberately degenerate ones, including one meant to over-approve. Told to be lax, the model refused to be bad and caught almost everything anyway. The gate declared the hypothesis "falsified" — but that was wrong: the control merely failed to become degenerate, which by the rules is inconclusive, not failure. Lesson: you can't make a bad reviewer just by telling a capable model to be bad.

2

The corroboration that was true, but too broad

automated verdict: "corroborated" — but overbroad

I replaced the control with a structurally broken one. This time the metric cleanly separated the good from the broken. But reading the raw data showed all discrimination came from one of the two numbers (false positives); the other (recall) was saturated and did no work. "The metric works" overstated what the data licensed. Lesson: a correct verdict can still be an overclaim of scope.

3.0

The control that collapsed into another control

automated verdict: "inconclusive" — hidden defect

I added the reviewer I most wanted to test: one that looks rigorous but attributes the wrong cause. Specified by instruction, it collapsed into flagging everything — identical to one I already had. The gate passed it on a technicality; only reading the per-case findings caught the collapse.

3.1

The result that was never real

automated verdict: "corroborated" — on fake data

I reran. The gate said "corroborated," with a beautiful recall number. It was simulation data dry-run = a test run with fake data, no real model call — a hardcoded value in the mock, presented as a real run. There was no model call at all. I caught it only by noticing every log row was flagged as simulation. Lesson: the most dangerous result is the one that confirms what you hoped.

3.1

The real verdict — and the control that still couldn't be built

verdict: inconclusive, but honest

The real run, finally clean. The contract reviewer beat the naive one on false positives by a wide margin, replicating the result on a second independent corpus. But recall saturated again (the model finds the cause however subtle the text), and the wrong-attribution reviewer couldn't be built even on the third attempt. Lesson: the most dangerous failure mode may be an emergent property of flawed reasoning, not something you can fabricate.

07

The finding that arrived sideways

Look again at the five automated-gate verdicts: falsified, corroborated, inconclusive, corroborated, inconclusive. In every run, the gate accepted a defective result — and in every run, only human audit of the raw data caught it. The gate caught what it was built to catch and passed every new failure mode.

The argument

This is structural, not a bug in my gate. A closed-rule gate can only check the failure modes its author anticipated. But the reason you run an experiment is precisely that the result isn't known in advance. The gate is complete only against the past; the process generates the future. So the newer and more interesting the defect, the more certain it is to escape.

And here's the recursion that makes this strong: CAA says the party that produces evidence cannot be the one that assures it. This study, while trying to validate something else, generated five independent demonstrations of exactly that principle. The independence of the auditor is not an implementation detail of CAA — in this study, it was the condition that caught every error that mattered.

08

The honest verdict

✓ Demonstrated

The paired metric separates the contract reviewer from the naive one, and both from two degenerates — on the false-positive axis, replicated across two independent corpora with real model calls. The core of the idea holds.

✗ Not demonstrated (with a known reason)

Recall-axis discrimination between plausible reviewers. Recall saturates on textual cases, reproducibly — a property of how models read prose, which I report as a limitation rather than hide.

⊘ Untested

Detection of the wrong-attribution reviewer — the most dangerous mode — because the control resisted three construction attempts. The repeated difficulty is a finding.

⚠ Refuted

That a closed-rule automated gate is sufficient on its own. It failed in all five runs; only human audit caught each defect. This is the strongest result, and it arrived sideways.

So, does it work? It works where I could test it, honestly maps where it doesn't, and by failing to police itself it proved its own central premise. For a framework whose entire pitch is "don't trust the reviewer, verify it" — there's no more fitting result.

It's a pilot

n = 20 pairs n = sample size, single author, one model, cases in Brazilian Portuguese. The conclusions are well-founded for a pilot, not for a definitive claim — and the documents say so everywhere. The strength is not statistical robustness; it's the honesty of scope and the recursive finding, which doesn't depend on sample size.

Glossary — every term in one place

CAA — Continuous Architecture Assurance: the whole framework, the subject of this document.

AI — Artificial Intelligence.

metrology — the science of measurement and instrument calibration.

oracle — a source believed on authority, without verification (the opposite of what CAA wants).

assurance — independent guarantee / vouching for quality.

recall — hit rate: of the real failures, how many were caught.

false positive (FP) — an alarm raised where there was no real danger.

Youden — summary index: recall minus false-positive (higher is better).

postmortem — a public report analyzing a real incident.

matched pair — failure case + healthy twin, derived from the same incident.

distractor — a plausible but false cause, planted to test reasoning.

taxonomy — the fixed set of 12 root-cause categories.

blinding — removing from the text any clue that gives away the answer.

falsify — in the scientific sense, to try to prove a hypothesis wrong.

dry-run — a test run with simulated data, no real model call.

degenerate — a reviewer built on purpose to fail in a known way.

L0–L4 — the layers of the assurance plane.

R1–R5 — the five reviewers tested (naive, under contract, blind, flag-everything, wrong-attribution).

n — sample size (here, 20 pairs per corpus).

Continuous Architecture Assurance — didactic version of the research program. For full detail: the technical report, the three papers (framework, empirical, meta) and the corpus datasheet accompany the repository. Every number here was reconstructed from raw data, independently of the experiment harness.
